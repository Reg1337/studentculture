Discover Marrakech

Step into the vibrant heart of Morocco with Marrakech, a city renowned for its rich history, stunning architecture, and bustling souks. From the majestic palaces to the lively markets, Marrakech offers a sensory overload of sights, sounds, and flavors.

1.2 million residents

Marrakech is one of Morocco's most prominent cities, attracting tourists from around the globe.

Numerous Universities

Marrakech is home to several esteemed educational institutions, contributing to its vibrant academic environment.

Landmarks

Discover the Koutoubia Mosque, Saadian Tombs, and more historical sites.

Top Landmarks to Visit

Koutoubia Mosque

The Koutoubia Mosque is Marrakech's largest mosque, known for its stunning 77-meter-tall minaret that dominates the skyline. Built in the 12th century, it serves as a fine example of Almohad architecture. Although non-Muslims cannot enter, the mosque's grandeur can be admired from the surrounding gardens and the nearby plaza.

Saadian Tombs

The Saadian Tombs in Marrakech, dating back to the 16th century, are a stunning example of Saadian dynasty artistry. Discovered in 1917, the tombs are known for their elaborate tilework, carved cedar wood, and intricate stucco designs. The "Royal Mausoleum" contains the graves of Sultan Ahmad al-Mansur and his family, featuring beautifully decorated marble and mosaics. Surrounded by peaceful gardens, the Saadian Tombs offer a glimpse into Morocco's rich history and artistic heritage.

Jemaa el-Fnaa

Jemaa el-Fnaa is Marrakech's central square and market place, famous for its vibrant atmosphere and dynamic street performances. By day, the square is bustling with food stalls and traditional crafts; by night, it transforms into a lively hub of entertainment with storytellers, musicians, and dancers. It's the heart of Marrakech’s cultural experience.

Moroccan Cuisine

Tagine

Tagine is a traditional Moroccan stew named after the earthenware pot it's cooked in. This dish can feature a variety of ingredients, including lamb, chicken, and vegetables, all slow-cooked with aromatic spices. The result is a flavorful and tender meal that reflects the rich culinary heritage of Morocco.

Couscous

Couscous is a staple Moroccan dish made from steamed semolina wheat granules. It’s often served with a variety of vegetables and meats, creating a hearty and satisfying meal. Traditionally enjoyed on Fridays and during special occasions, couscous is a cornerstone of Moroccan cuisine.

Harira

Harira is a traditional Moroccan soup made with tomatoes, lentils, chickpeas, and a blend of spices. It’s commonly enjoyed during Ramadan to break the fast, offering a nourishing and flavorful start to the evening meal. The soup is rich and satisfying, often served with dates and bread.

Moroccan Traditions

Marrakech International Film Festival

Held annually, the Marrakech International Film Festival celebrates cinema from around the world. This prestigious event attracts filmmakers, actors, and film enthusiasts to enjoy screenings, awards, and cultural exchanges. It's a significant highlight in Marrakech's cultural calendar.

Festival of Popular Arts

The Festival of Popular Arts showcases Moroccan culture through music, dance, and traditional performances. Held in the heart of Marrakech, this vibrant festival features folk music, traditional dances, and colorful costumes, offering an immersive experience of Moroccan heritage.

Mint Tea Ceremony

The Moroccan mint tea ceremony is a beloved tradition that showcases Moroccan hospitality and the cultural significance of tea in Moroccan society. This ritual involves preparing a special blend of green tea, fresh mint leaves, and sugar, which is poured from a height to create a frothy top. Tea is served in small glasses and often accompanied by nuts, dried fruits, or pastries. The ceremony is not just about drinking tea; it’s a social event that fosters conversation and friendship. It reflects the Moroccan values of generosity and warmth, and is an essential part of Moroccan daily life and special occasions.
